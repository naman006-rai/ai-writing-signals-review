# Before / After Revisions (Cleanup Mode)

Worked examples for Prose Cleanup Mode. Each shows a generic pattern tightened into clearer, more specific prose. These are original examples written for this skill.

> These rewrites improve clarity and specificity. They are not proof of AI authorship and are not a way to evade detection. Every "after" preserves the original meaning and invents no new facts.

---

## 1. Throat-clearing + vague declarative

**Before:**
> Here's the thing: onboarding is where the implications are significant. The truth is, it sets the tone for everything that follows.

**After:**
> Onboarding shapes how new hires learn the team's tools, expectations, and first-week priorities.

**What changed:** Cut the opener and replaced "implications are significant" with the specific kind of influence already implied by the original. If the writer wants to claim measurable outcomes, they should add real data rather than inventing it.

---

## 2. Business jargon

**Before:**
> We need to leverage our core competencies and lean into the evolving landscape to move the needle.

**After:**
> We should use the team's existing strengths to improve the outcome we care about most.

**What changed:** Replaced interchangeable jargon with plain language without inventing a strength, metric, or business result. A stronger final version would name those specifics only if the writer can verify them.

---

## 3. False agency (inanimate actor)

**Before:**
> The complaint becomes a fix, and the culture shifts toward quality.

**After:**
> A complaint can improve quality only when someone reviews it, fixes the issue, and changes the process that allowed it.

**What changed:** Removed false agency without pretending to know which team acted or what process changed. A final edit should name the actor only if the source text or context provides it.

---

## 4. Manufactured drama (fragmentation + binary contrast)

**Before:**
> Speed. Quality. Cost. That's it. The problem isn't effort. It's focus.

**After:**
> You can usually optimize two of speed, quality, and cost at once. Most teams lose time by trying for all three.

**What changed:** Restored complete sentences and stated the point without the staccato setup/reveal.

---

## 5. Adverbs and intensifiers (overuse, not a ban)

**Before:**
> This is genuinely, really important, and it will literally change how you fundamentally work.

**After:**
> This changes one thing in your workflow: you review pull requests before, not after, the daily standup.

**What changed:** Dropped the stacked intensifiers and named the actual change. *Note:* the goal is reducing overuse — a single well-placed adverb is fine; the fix here is the empty stack, not the existence of adverbs.

---

## 6. When NOT to "fix" a pattern (false positive)

**Before (academic context):**
> The samples were incubated for 48 hours and then analyzed by two independent reviewers.

**Leave as-is.** This passive construction is correct: the samples (the object) are the topic, and the actor is either unimportant or named later. Forcing "We incubated the samples…" can be wrong for the venue. Patterns in the watchlist are defaults to check, not rules to apply blindly.
